### **Table Trends to Match**

**1. Overall Performance Across Imbalance Ratios (Table 1 Benchmark)**

* 
**The Baseline Drop:** As you increase the imbalance ratio of your dataset (e.g., from 1 to 20, 50, and 100), your Base Model's overall accuracy should drop significantly.


* 
**Consistent Superiority:** Your implementation of "Our Method" should consistently achieve higher overall accuracy than both the Base Model and standard Class-Balance Loss across all imbalanced scenarios (ratios 20, 50, and 100).


* 
**The Balanced Baseline:** If you run an experiment with an imbalance ratio of 1 (a perfectly balanced dataset), your method should perform nearly identically to the Base Model, as no extreme re-weighting is necessary.



**2. Majority vs. Minority Class Dynamics (Table 2 Benchmark)**

* 
**The Baseline Bias:** When looking at the Base Model, the accuracy on Majority classes should be vastly higher than on Minority classes.


* 
**The Class-Balance Trade-off:** If you test standard Class-Balance loss, you should see Minority class accuracy increase, but Majority class accuracy will decrease compared to the Base Model.


* 
**The Instance-Level Win:** Your implementation should simultaneously improve the accuracy of **both** the Majority classes and the Minority classes compared to the Base Model. This proves your method is finding the "hard" instances even within the majority groups.



**3. Sub-Class/Hidden Imbalance Correction (Table 3 Benchmark)**

* 
**Reduced Ratios:** If you calculate the effective Imbalance Ratio based on your dynamic sampling weights, you should see both the overall Class Ratio and the hidden Sub-Class Ratio decrease significantly compared to the raw dataset distribution.


* 
**Minority Sub-Class Boost:** Your accuracy on the hidden minority sub-classes should see a massive improvement compared to the Base Model.
