

### **1. Detailed Analysis & Core Concept**

**The Problem:**
Real-world datasets are often class-imbalanced, where a few majority classes dominate the training data. Existing solutions (like Focal Loss or Class-Balance Loss) typically re-balance data at the *class level* by assigning higher weights to minority classes and lower weights to majority classes. However, this coarse approach ignores that even majority classes contain "difficult" instances. When class-level weights are reduced for the majority class, these inherently difficult instances become even harder to learn, hurting overall model performance.

**The Proposed Solution:**
The authors propose an **instance-level re-balancing strategy** based on "instance difficulty". Inspired by human learning, they measure difficulty by tracking an instance's learning speed during training. Specifically, they monitor whether an instance is experiencing "learning" (loss decreasing) or "unlearning" (loss increasing/predictions getting worse) between training epochs. Instances that are frequently "unlearned" are deemed harder and are assigned higher sampling probabilities in subsequent epochs.

---

### **2. Mathematical Formulation**

The core of the paper relies on calculating the prediction variation between the current epoch $t$ and the previous epoch $t-1$ to determine the learning and unlearning trends.

**a) Prediction Definitions**
Let $p_{i,t}^j$ be the probability that the model at epoch $t$ predicts instance $i$ belongs to class $j$.
Let $y_i$ be the true ground-truth class for instance $i$.

**b) Unlearning Trend ($du_{i,t}$)**
This captures how much the model has "forgotten" or worsened on instance $i$ between epochs. It is defined as:


$$du_{i,t} = \min(p_{i,t}^{y_i} - p_{i,t-1}^{y_i}, 0)\ln\left(\frac{p_{i,t}^{y_i}}{p_{i,t-1}^{y_i}}\right) + \sum_{j=1, j\neq y_i}^k \max(p_{i,t}^j - p_{i,t-1}^j, 0)\ln\left(\frac{p_{i,t}^j}{p_{i,t-1}^j}\right)$$



**c) Learning Trend ($dl_{i,t}$)**
This captures how much the model has improved on instance $i$ between epochs:


$$dl_{i,t} = \max(p_{i,t}^{y_i} - p_{i,t-1}^{y_i}, 0)\ln\left(\frac{p_{i,t}^{y_i}}{p_{i,t-1}^{y_i}}\right) + \sum_{j=1, j\neq y_i}^k \min(p_{i,t}^j - p_{i,t-1}^j, 0)\ln\left(\frac{p_{i,t}^j}{p_{i,t-1}^j}\right)$$



**d) Cumulative Instance Difficulty ($D_{i,T}$)**
After $T$ iterations, the total difficulty for instance $i$ is the ratio of accumulated unlearning to accumulated learning, smoothed by a prior constant $c$:


$$D_{i,T} = \frac{c + \sum_{t=1}^T du_{i,t}}{c + \sum_{t=1}^T dl_{i,t}}$$



**e) Sampling Weight ($w_{i,t}$)**
The actual probability of sampling instance $i$ at epoch $t$ is its normalized difficulty:


$$w_{i,t} = \frac{D_{i,t}}{\sum_{j=1}^N D_{j,t}}$$



---

### **3. Implementation Logic (Algorithm 1)**

The training loop incorporates dynamic resampling based on the math above:

1. 
**Initialize:** * Set initial sampling weights $w$ uniformly for all instances in dataset $S$: $w \leftarrow \{1 / |S|\}$.


* Initialize base prediction probabilities $p_{i,0} \leftarrow \{1/k, ...\}$ (uniform distribution across $k$ classes) for each instance.




2. **For each training epoch $t$ from 1 to $T$:**
* 
**Sample:** Create a new training subset $S^*$ by sampling from $S$ according to the current probability weights $w$.


* 
**Train:** Train the network using subset $S^*$ for one epoch.


* 
**Evaluate:** Pass the *entire* original dataset $S$ through the updated network (without calculating gradients) to get new prediction probabilities: $p_{i,t} \leftarrow Net(x_i)$.


* **Update Difficulty:** For each instance, calculate $du_{i,t}$ and $dl_{i,t}$ using $p_{i,t}$ and $p_{i,t-1}$. Update the cumulative difficulty $D_{i,t}$.


* 
**Update Weights:** Recalculate the sampling probability array $w$ using the new $D_{i,t}$ values.

### **4. Evaluation Text & Results**

The paper validates this approach across multiple benchmarks, proving its superiority over class-level methods:

* 
**Long-Tailed CIFAR-10/-100:** Tested across imbalance ratios of 1, 20, 50, and 100. The proposed method outperformed the Base Model, Focal Loss, and Class-Balance Loss across almost all imbalanced scenarios. For instance, on CIFAR-100 (Ratio 100), the overall accuracy reached 42.3%, compared to the base model's 40.6% and Class-Balance Loss's 41.1%.


* 
**Minority vs. Majority Classes:** Unlike Class-Balance Loss (which improved minority classes at the heavy expense of majority classes), the proposed instance-level method successfully improved performance on *both* majority (56.2%) and minority (9.9%) classes compared to the base model (54.1% and 9.0% respectively).


* 
**Sub-Class Simulation (Long-Tailed MNIST):** The researchers created an Even vs. Odd binary classification task where the underlying digits (sub-classes) were imbalanced. The method effectively identified difficult minority sub-classes (like the digit '8' inside the 'Even' class) and dynamically reduced the sub-class imbalance ratio from 100.0 to 37.3.


* 
**Generality:** Tested on 10 UCI datasets (e.g., Sonar, Balance, Iris) using Logistic Regression. The method achieved an average accuracy of 78.2%, outperforming both the Base model (74.9%) and Class-Balance Loss (76.4%).
